# Module 12: Category Theory - Structure-Preserving Systems

## Core Question

What is common to all structure-preserving mathematics?

## Limitation

After learning groups, vector spaces, rings, fields, and topological spaces, we see the same pattern repeatedly: objects and maps that preserve structure.

## New Object

A category: objects plus arrows between objects, with associative composition and identity arrows.

## Legal Operations

- Compare whole mathematical worlds.
- Study structure-preserving maps.
- Define universal properties.
- Use functors to translate between categories.
- Use natural transformations to compare translations.

## Invariants

- Isomorphism
- Universal property
- Commutative diagrams
- Limits and colimits
- Equivalence of categories

## Concrete Example

Vector spaces with linear maps form a category. Groups with group homomorphisms form a category. The pattern is not the same objects; the pattern is structure-preserving arrows.

## Hand Exercises

1. In the category of sets, what are the objects and arrows?
2. In the category of vector spaces, what are the objects and arrows?
3. Explain why an isomorphism means "same structure" rather than "same presentation."

## Depends On

Functions, composition, algebraic structures, and comfort with abstraction.

## Supports Later

Algebraic topology, algebraic geometry, type theory, abstract algebra, and structural mathematics.
